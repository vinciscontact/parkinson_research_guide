The one-sentence thesis

If oxidative stress drives the Parkinson's-like damage rotenone causes, then an antioxidant molecule that can enter the fly's system should measurably reduce that damage — and you can see it in how well the flies climb, how much antioxidant enzyme activity survives, and how long they live.

Playbook 01 · The reasoning

The science you'll be asked to defend.

Every choice in this project answers a "why". Get these four right and the rest of the study is just careful execution.

Why 1 · The diseaseWhat Parkinson's actually is here

Parkinson's disease is the progressive death of dopaminergic neurons in a midbrain region called the substantia nigra. Dopamine is the neurotransmitter that lets the brain smoothly initiate and control movement, so as those neurons die you get the classic motor signs — tremor, rigidity, slowness (bradykinesia), and postural instability.

Two mechanisms sit at the centre of why the neurons die, and both are things you can actually measure:

Oxidative stress

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) build up faster than the cell's antioxidant defences can neutralise them. They damage lipids, proteins and DNA. Dopaminergic neurons are unusually vulnerable because dopamine metabolism itself generates ROS.

Neuroinflammation

Glial cells become chronically activated, releasing inflammatory mediators that add to the injury. It amplifies and sustains the damage started by oxidative stress.

Why it matters for you

Because oxidative stress is central, an antioxidant compound is a mechanistically sensible thing to test. Your whole hypothesis rides on this link — that's why you measure antioxidant enzymes (SOD, CAT, GSH) as your biochemical proof.

Why 2 · The triggerWhy rotenone builds a Parkinson's model

Rotenone is a naturally occurring pesticide that blocks Complex I of the mitochondrial electron-transport chain. Blocking Complex I does two things at once: it starves the cell of ATP and it causes electrons to leak and form ROS. That combination selectively injures dopaminergic neurons and reproduces both the motor deficits and the oxidative stress signature of Parkinson's — which is exactly why it's a standard PD model (this is the model established in your cited Coulom & Birman, 2004).

The elegance of it

Rotenone doesn't just "make flies sick" — it makes them sick through the same mechanism your drug is supposed to counter. Injury by oxidation, rescue by antioxidant. That mechanistic match is what makes the experiment interpretable.

Why 3 · The animalWhy Drosophila melanogaster

  • It has a real dopaminergic system and a brain — so rotenone produces genuine, measurable motor decline, not just toxicity.
  • Fast and cheap — a full generation in ~10 days, hundreds of animals, minimal cost. Perfect for a student project on a real budget.
  • ~75% of human disease genes have a fly counterpart — findings translate meaningfully toward mammalian work.
  • Simple, quantitative behaviour — the climbing (negative geotaxis) assay gives you a clean number for motor function.
  • Ethically light — invertebrate work sidesteps the heavy regulatory load of rodent studies.

Why 4 · The drugWhy isocoumarins from shiitake

Shiitake (Lentinus edodes) is famous for its polysaccharide lentinan — but polysaccharides are large and hard to deliver. Recent work (your cited Xu et al., 2024) found a different class of small-molecule secondary metabolites in shiitake: isocoumarins such as the hydroxymelleins and lentincoumarins. These carry notable antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activity.

Why "small" is the whole point

Small molecules can cross membranes and potentially the blood–brain barrier in ways big polysaccharides can't. If a small, cheap, food-derived antioxidant works, it's a far more promising lead for a real "functional food" or drug. That's the novelty your project is built on — and your best answer to "so what?"

Honest limitation to state up front

A fly is not a human. This is a screening study — proof-of-concept that a compound is worth taking into mammalian models. Framing it this way (rather than over-claiming a cure) is what makes it credible science.

Playbook 02 · The blueprint

Turning the aim into a design you can actually run.

A project fails or passes here. Before touching a fly, lock down your hypotheses, your groups, and your variables.

From objective to hypothesisState it so it can be proven wrong

Null hypothesis (H₀)

Shiitake isocoumarin treatment has no effect on rotenone-induced motor decline, oxidative-stress markers, or lifespan in Drosophila.

Alternative hypothesis (H₁)

Isocoumarin treatment significantly improves climbing, restores antioxidant enzyme activity, lowers lipid peroxidation, and extends lifespan versus rotenone-only flies — in a dose-dependent way.

The experimental groupsSeven groups — and why each exists

Every group answers a specific "what if someone objects that…". Missing groups are the number-one reason a viva panel picks a project apart.

#GroupDietThe objection it defeats
1CTRL Untreated controlNormal food"What does a healthy fly even look like?" — your baseline.
2MODEL Rotenone onlyFood + rotenone"Did the disease model even work?" — your positive damage control.
3TX-Lo Rotenone + low doseRotenone + iso (low)"Is any effect real and does more drug do more?" — dose–response.
4TX-Mid Rotenone + mid doseRotenone + iso (mid)

Design tip

Also include a vehicle control: if you dissolve your isocoumarin in a trace of DMSO or ethanol, one group must get rotenone + that same solvent with no drug, so any effect can't be blamed on the solvent.

VariablesName them explicitly — examiners ask

Independent

Presence of rotenone; isocoumarin dose (0 / low / mid / high).

Dependent (measured)

% climbing success; SOD, CAT, GSH activity; MDA level; dopamine content; survival.

Controlled (kept constant)

Temperature (25 °C), 12:12 light cycle, humidity, fly age & sex, food batch, handling time.

Statistical powerHow many flies, and how many times

  • ≥ 3 biological replicates per group — three independent vials set up on different days, not three counts of one vial.
  • ~20–30 flies per replicate vial for behaviour; pool ~20–30 flies per replicate for each biochemical assay (you need enough tissue).
  • Age- and sex-matched adults (commonly males, 3–5 days old) to remove those as confounders.
  • Blind your scoring where possible — code the vials so the person counting climbing doesn't know the group.
Rule of thumb

7 groups × 3 replicates × ~30 flies ≈ ~630 flies per full run for behaviour, plus separate cohorts for the biochemistry (which is destructive — you homogenise the flies). Budget your fly numbers before you start.

Playbook 03 · Track A

Making the molecule and proving what it is.

This track produces a small amount of purified, identified isocoumarin. "Identified" is doing heavy lifting — a reviewer will not accept "we extracted something antioxidant." You must show what the molecule is and how pure it is.

  1. Culture the mycelium

    Grow Lentinus edodes mycelium in liquid culture — e.g. potato-dextrose broth or malt extract broth — in shake flasks, sterile, ~25 °C, for 2–4 weeks. Mycelium is used (not fruiting bodies) because it's faster to grow and is where these secondary metabolites are produced. Harvest by filtration.

  2. Extract the crude compounds

    Freeze-dry (lyophilise) the mycelial biomass, then do a solvent extraction. Isocoumarins are mid-polarity, so ethyl acetate or methanol (often a sequential polarity series: hexane → ethyl acetate → methanol) pulls them out. Filter, then concentrate on a rotary evaporator under reduced pressure to get a crude extract.

  3. Fractionate by column chromatography

    Load the crude extract onto a silica-gel column and elute with a gradient (e.g. increasing ethyl acetate in hexane). This separates the crude mixture into fractions by polarity. Track fractions with thin-layer chromatography (TLC) — spots under UV tell you which fractions contain your target.

  4. Purify to a single compound (HPLC)

    Take the isocoumarin-containing fractions and polish them on preparative reverse-phase HPLC (a C18 column, water/acetonitrile or water/methanol gradient). A single sharp peak = a pure compound. This is the purity step your project explicitly calls for.

  5. Confirm identity & purity (the proof)

    Now identify what you actually isolated, using complementary methods — no single one is enough:

MethodWhat it tells youWhat you look for
Analytical HPLCPurityOne sharp, symmetric peak = pure. Multiple peaks = still a mixture.
LC-MS (mass spec)Molecular weightAn m/z matching the known isocoumarin (e.g. a hydroxymellein) confirms the mass.
NMR (¹H & ¹³C)Full chemical structureThe proton/carbon environments that map onto the isocoumarin scaffold — the definitive ID.
UV-VisChromophore fingerprintAn absorbance maximum characteristic of the isocoumarin ring system.
FTIRFunctional groupsLactone C=O stretch, O–H, aromatic C=C — consistent with the isocoumarin.
Why so many instruments

Mass alone can't distinguish isomers; UV/IR alone can't give a structure. The convincing story is: HPLC says pure → MS says the right mass → NMR says the right structure → UV/IR agree. That layered evidence is what turns "an extract" into "compound X, ≥95% pure."

Reality check for a student lab

Prep-HPLC, LC-MS and NMR are shared/expensive instruments. If access is limited, a legitimate scaled-down version is: extract → column + TLC → analytical HPLC for purity → send one clean fraction out for MS/NMR confirmation. If you genuinely can't purify a single compound, you can still run the biology on a characterised standardised fraction and say so honestly — but a pure compound is the stronger result.

Shortcut worth knowing

If purifying enough material from mycelium is hard, some isocoumarins are commercially available as authentic standards, or can be used to spike/confirm your extract. Using a standard to confirm your peak's identity (co-elution) is a recognised, defensible method.

Playbook 04 · Track B

Building the disease, then delivering the drug.

This track turns healthy flies into a Parkinson's model and gets your compound into them — all through the food they eat.

  1. Rear a synchronised fly stock

    Maintain a wild-type stock (commonly Canton-S or Oregon-R) on standard cornmeal–agar–yeast medium at 25 °C, 60% humidity, 12:12 light:dark. Collect newly-eclosed adults and age-synchronise them; typically use males, 3–5 days old, so age and sex aren't confounders.

  2. Prepare drug-laced food

    Melt standard fly food, cool to ~50–55 °C, then mix in the required chemicals so they're evenly distributed as the food sets. Everything is delivered orally, which is realistic (a "functional food" route) and avoids injection artefacts. Make each group's food as a separate batch.

  3. Induce the Parkinson's phenotype with rotenone

    Add rotenone to the food of the model and treatment groups. Typical range in the fly literature is ~250–500 µM in the food (the Coulom & Birman model), fed for around 7 days. Confirm the model "took" by checking that rotenone-only flies climb significantly worse than untreated controls before you trust any rescue.

  4. Co-administer the isocoumarin at graded doses

    Treatment groups get rotenone plus isocoumarin at low / mid / high concentrations (see the Chemicals tab for how to set these). Feeding is usually concurrent — the drug is present alongside the toxin so you're testing protection. Transfer flies to fresh food every 2–3 days so doses stay consistent.

  5. Run a dose-tolerability pre-screen first

    Before the main experiment, feed healthy flies the isocoumarin alone across a wide dose range and watch survival for a few days. This finds the non-toxic ceiling and sets your "high" dose sensibly — you never want your "high" dose to be killing flies through toxicity rather than disease.

Timing map

Day 0: sort flies into groups on their assigned food. Days 1–7: feeding period (refresh food, log any deaths daily for the lifespan curve). Day 7 (or at defined endpoints): run behavioural assays, then harvest flies for biochemistry.

Handling matters

Anaesthetise flies gently (CO₂ pad) and let them fully recover before any climbing assay — anaesthesia itself impairs movement and will corrupt your motor data. Count deaths at the same time each day.

Playbook 05 · The readouts

The tests that turn flies into numbers.

Three families of readout, each answering a different question: behaviour (is function preserved?), biochemistry (is oxidative damage reduced?), survival (does it help them live longer?).

Readout 1 · BehaviourThe climbing (negative geotaxis) assay

Flies instinctively climb upward against gravity when tapped down — a behaviour that depends on an intact dopaminergic motor system. PD-model flies climb poorly, so climbing is a direct, quantitative motor readout.

How to run it

Put ~20–30 flies in a vertical vial marked with a line ~8 cm up. Tap them sharply to the bottom, then count the % of flies that cross the line within a set time (e.g. 10 s). Repeat several trials per vial and average.

What "rescue" looks like

Control ≈ high climbing. Rotenone-only = sharply reduced. A working dose pushes the treated group's climbing back up toward control — ideally more at higher doses.

Pro tip

Record it on video and count frame-by-frame, or use free tracking software. It removes human bias and lets you also measure climbing speed, a more sensitive metric than pass/fail.

Readout 2 · BiochemistryThe oxidative-stress panel

This is where you prove the mechanism. Homogenise a pooled batch of flies from each group, spin down debris, and run these on the supernatant. Always normalise to total protein (Bradford or Lowry assay) so you compare like with like.

Read the pattern, not single numbers

The convincing biochemistry story is internally consistent: rotenone drops SOD/CAT/GSH and raises MDA (defences down, damage up). A working antioxidant should reverse all four together. If only one marker moves, be cautious in your claims.

Readout 3 · SurvivalThe lifespan assay

Keep separate cohorts per group and record deaths daily until all flies die. Plot Kaplan–Meier survival curves. Rotenone shortens lifespan; effective treatment shifts the curve rightward (flies live longer). This is your whole-organism proof that the molecular effect translates into a real benefit.

What good data looks like together

Climbing improves + antioxidant enzymes recover + MDA falls + flies live longer — all moving the same direction, ideally scaling with dose. That convergence across independent readouts is far more persuasive than any one graph.

The one idea that unlocks all assays

Every biochemical readout follows the same logic: an invisible molecule you care about is converted into a coloured product you can see, and the depth of colour (absorbance) is proportional to how much was there. The spectrophotometer just turns that colour into a number you can graph and run statistics on.

Safety, briefly

Rotenone is a toxin — handle with gloves, in a fume hood, dispose per protocol, and never mouth-pipette. Organic solvents are flammable and need ventilation. Follow your lab's SOPs and get sign-off from your supervisor before starting.

Playbook 07 · Making sense of it

From raw numbers to a defensible conclusion.

Good data collection is wasted without the right analysis. Here's what to graph, which test to run, and how to know if your result is real.

Match the graph to the readoutWhat to plot

ReadoutBest plotReading it
Climbing %Bar chart (± SEM), groups side by sideLook for treated bars sitting between model and control, rising with dose.
SOD / CAT / GSH / MDAGrouped bar charts per markerAntioxidants recover; MDA drops. Consistency across markers = a real effect.
Dose–responseLine/scatter: effect vs. doseA rising trend supports true pharmacology, not a fluke.
SurvivalKaplan–Meier curvesA rightward shift = longer life. Compare curves statistically.

Choosing the testWhich statistic, and why

  • One-way ANOVA to compare the means across your 7 groups (climbing, each biochemical marker). ANOVA first tells you "is any group different?"
  • Post-hoc test (Tukey's HSD, or Dunnett's if comparing every group back to one control) to find which groups differ — e.g. is TX-Hi significantly better than MODEL?
  • Log-rank (Mantel–Cox) test to compare Kaplan–Meier survival curves.
  • Check assumptions (normality, equal variance); if they fail, use non-parametric equivalents (Kruskal–Wallis).
  • Report mean ± SEM, the test used, and the exact p-value. Treat p < 0.05 as significant, and mark it on graphs (* <0.05, ** <0.01, *** <0.001).
Tools

GraphPad Prism is the standard and does all of this with guided menus. Free alternatives: R (with ggplot2), or Python (pandas + scipy + seaborn). Even a careful spreadsheet handles ANOVA for a project this size.

Reading the result honestlyWhat each outcome means

Clear rescue

Treated groups significantly better than model, scaling with dose, across multiple readouts. → Reject H₀. Strong result.

Partial rescue

Some markers move, others don't; only high dose works. → Real but nuanced — report it as such, don't overstate.

No effect

Treated ≈ model. → Fail to reject H₀. This is still a valid, publishable finding — negative results matter.

The credibility test

Ask of your own data: does the story hold across independent readouts? Behaviour, biochemistry and survival pointing the same way is far harder to dismiss than one lucky graph. Convergent evidence is the whole game.

Feasibility & costWhere the money and risk sit

Cheap & low-risk

Fly rearing, food prep, climbing assay, spectrophotometric biochemistry (SOD/CAT/GSH/MDA), DPPH. This is the affordable, robust core of the project — and where most of your data comes from.

Costly / access-dependent

Prep-HPLC, LC-MS, NMR, HPLC-ECD dopamine. Book instrument time early; have a fallback (outsource one confirmation run, or use an authentic standard) so a booking delay doesn't sink the timeline.

De-risk it

Run the fly pipeline once end-to-end with a known antioxidant (e.g. your reference group's compound) as a rehearsal before your precious purified isocoumarin arrives. You'll debug the assays on cheap material and be fast & confident when it counts.

Playbook 09 · Alternatives

Other legitimate ways to run this.

The proposal describes one solid route. But a strong viva answer shows you know the alternatives and why you chose what you chose. Here are the main forks.

Fork 1Getting the compound

RouteProsCons
Extract from mycelium (the plan)Novel, self-contained, "from the mushroom"Low yield, needs purification & expensive ID
Fruiting-body extractionMore biomass availableSlower to grow, may differ in metabolites
Buy an authentic standardPure, defined, fast, reproducibleLess "discovery"; loses the extraction story
Standardised fraction (not fully pure)Faster; still biologically testableWeaker mechanistic claim — must characterise it

Fork 2Building the Parkinson's model

  • Rotenone-fed flies (the plan) — cheap, oxidative-stress mechanism matches your antioxidant hypothesis perfectly. Best fit here.
  • Paraquat — another oxidative-stress toxin; a good confirmatory second model if you want to show the effect isn't rotenone-specific.
  • Genetic PD flies (e.g. parkin or α-synuclein mutants) — mimic inherited PD; powerful but needs specific fly lines and more expertise.
  • Scaling up to rodents — the natural "next paper," not a student first project: costly, slow, heavy ethics. Your fly result is the justification for someone doing this later.

Fork 3Adding depth to the readouts

  • Dopaminergic neuron imaging — immunostain the fly brain for tyrosine hydroxylase and count surviving dopaminergic neurons. Direct anatomical proof of neuroprotection.
  • Gene-expression (qPCR) — measure antioxidant/inflammatory genes to show how protection works, not just that it happens.
  • Extra behaviour — startle-induced climbing speed, or activity monitors, for finer motor resolution.
  • Molecular docking (in silico) — model how your isocoumarin binds antioxidant-pathway targets; cheap, adds a mechanistic chapter, no wet lab.
How to present the choice

Frame your chosen route as the sweet spot of novelty × feasibility × mechanistic fit: mycelial extraction gives you a discovery angle, the rotenone model matches your antioxidant hypothesis, and the fly system keeps it affordable. Knowing the alternatives is exactly what separates a strong project from a recipe followed blindly.
